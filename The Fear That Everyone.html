An Essay on Fear & Love
The Fear That Everyone Will Eventually Leave
By Princess February 2026  ·  7 min read
Read
You don't fully trust anyone who loves you.

Not because they've done anything wrong. Not because they've given you reasons to doubt. But because deep down, you're waiting for them to leave.

You're waiting for the moment they realize you're not worth it. Not worth the effort. Not worth staying for. Not worth the trouble of loving.

You're waiting for them to see the real you and decide it's too much or not enough or just not what they signed up for.

So you don't relax into love. You don't fully believe it. You don't let yourself feel safe.

Because everyone leaves eventually. That's what you learned. That's what you know. That's what you're preparing for.

Maybe someone left when you were young. A parent. A person who was supposed to stay no matter what. And their leaving taught you that love is conditional. That people leave. That nobody really stays.

Maybe it was a pattern. Person after person. Relationship after relationship. Friend after friend. They all left. They all proved that connection is temporary. That people give up. That you're not someone worth staying for.

Maybe you were abandoned in ways that were quiet. Not dramatic exits. Just slow fading. People who didn't leave but stopped showing up. Stopped caring. Stopped trying. Present but absent. There but not really there.

And you learned: This is what happens. People leave. In one way or another. Eventually. Always.

✦

So now you live with that fear. That certainty. That knowing that everyone you love will eventually leave you.

And it affects everything. Every relationship. Every connection. Every moment of closeness.

You sabotage. You push people away before they can leave you. You test them. You create problems to see if they'll stay. You make it hard to love you so you can prove your fear is justified.

You keep walls up. You don't let people get too close. You hold back parts of yourself. You never fully let anyone in because letting them in means it will hurt more when they leave.

You're hypervigilant. You watch for signs. You analyze everything. Every word. Every action. Every change in tone or energy. Looking for proof that they're about to leave. That this is the moment. That you were right all along.

You overfunction. You give too much. You try too hard. You make yourself indispensable. Because maybe if you're helpful enough, important enough, perfect enough, maybe they won't leave. Maybe you can earn their staying.

You underfunction. You become small. You don't ask for anything. You don't express needs. You don't risk being too much. Because maybe if you're easy, if you don't demand anything, if you're not a burden, maybe they won't have a reason to leave.

You anticipate the end. In every good moment, you're already imagining how it will feel when it's over.

You can't fully enjoy the present because you're preparing for the loss. You're already grieving while they're still here.

You misinterpret everything. They need space and you hear "I'm leaving." They have a bad day and you think "they're done with me." They're quiet and you assume "they've realized I'm not worth it."

You apologize constantly. For existing. For needing. For feeling. For being yourself. Because maybe if you apologize enough, they won't get frustrated enough to leave.

✦

And the tragedy is: Sometimes your fear creates what you're afraid of. Your constant waiting for them to leave makes them feel like you don't trust them. Your walls make them feel shut out. Your testing exhausts them. Your assumption that they'll leave becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy.

They leave. Not because you weren't worth staying for. But because your fear made the relationship impossible. Because loving someone who's constantly waiting for you to leave is exhausting.

And when they go, you say "See? I knew it. Everyone leaves. I was right."

But you weren't right. You were scared. And your fear pushed away someone who might have stayed.

This is the cruelest part of abandonment wounds. They make you create the very abandonment you fear.

They turn your relationships into self-fulfilling prophecies. They make staying impossible even for people who want to stay.

✦

So what do you do? How do you stop living in constant fear that everyone will leave?

You start by acknowledging the fear. By seeing it. By recognizing that this fear is based on past hurt, not present reality. That not everyone is the person who left. That not every relationship will end the same way.

You start by challenging the thoughts. When your brain says "they're going to leave," you ask "is that true or is that my fear talking?" When you interpret distance as abandonment, you ask "is there another explanation?"

You start by communicating. Instead of testing people or pushing them away or waiting for them to figure out what's wrong, you tell them. "I'm scared you're going to leave. I know that's my fear. But it's affecting how I show up. Can we talk about it?"

You start by letting people prove they'll stay. Not by testing them. But by being vulnerable and letting them respond. By sharing your fear and seeing what they do with it. By giving them the chance to reassure you, to show you, to stay.

You start by understanding that some people will leave. And that's okay. Not everyone is meant to stay forever. Some relationships have natural endings. Some people grow apart. Some connections run their course.

And that doesn't mean you're unworthy. It doesn't mean love isn't real. It doesn't mean you should never trust anyone. It just means that loss is part of life. And surviving loss doesn't mean preventing all future loss by never letting anyone in.

You start by taking risks. Small ones. You let someone in a little more. You trust a little more. You relax into love a little more. And you survive it. Even if they eventually leave, you survive. And that teaches you that you can handle loss without having to prevent all connection.

You start by healing the original wound. The first person who left. The relationship that taught you everyone leaves. You grieve it. You feel it. You stop running from that pain and start processing it so it stops controlling your present.

You start by being the person who stays. For yourself.

You stop abandoning yourself when things get hard. You stop leaving yourself when you're struggling. You become the steady presence you needed from others.

And slowly, gradually, painfully, you learn that not everyone leaves. Some people stay. Some people fight for the relationship. Some people see you at your worst and choose you anyway.

Some people prove that love doesn't always end. That connection can last. That you are someone worth staying for.

✦

But you have to let them. You have to stop pushing them away. You have to stop testing them. You have to stop assuming they'll leave and start believing they might stay.

That's terrifying. Because what if you believe and they leave anyway? What if you let yourself trust and they break that trust? What if you open up and they abandon you?

Those are possibilities. Real possibilities. Love always comes with the risk of loss. Connection always comes with the chance of abandonment. Trust always includes the possibility of betrayal.

But the alternative is worse. The alternative is living your whole life behind walls. Never letting anyone in. Never trusting anyone fully. Never relaxing into love. Always waiting for the end.

That's not living. That's just surviving. That's existing in constant fear instead of experiencing actual connection.

So take the risk. Let people stay. Believe they might. Trust them. Open up. Be vulnerable.

And if they leave? You'll survive. You've survived every other loss. You'll survive that one too.

But if they stay? If they prove that your fear was wrong? If they show you that some people do stay, do keep choosing you, do keep loving you?

That's worth the risk. That's worth the fear. That's worth the vulnerability.

Because you deserve to experience love without constantly waiting for it to end. You deserve to trust without always expecting betrayal. You deserve to connect without always preparing for abandonment.

You deserve relationships where you can relax. Where you can be yourself. Where you don't have to be perfect or easy or helpful enough to earn staying.

You deserve people who stay because they want to. Because they love you. Because you're worth staying for.

✦

And you are. You've always been. Not everyone saw it. Not everyone stayed. But that doesn't mean you're not worth staying for.

It just means they weren't your people. It means the relationship wasn't meant to last forever. It means the loss was about circumstances, compatibility, timing, not about your worth.

Your worth isn't determined by who stayed or left. It's inherent. It's always been there. It's always been enough.

So stop waiting for everyone to leave. Stop preparing for abandonment. Stop assuming every relationship will end.

Start believing some people stay. Start trusting some love lasts. Start opening up to the possibility that you are someone worth staying for.

Not everyone will see it.

Not everyone will stay.

But some will.

Let them. Trust them. Believe them.

And let yourself finally experience what it feels like
to be loved without waiting for it to end.

You deserve that.

You always have.

You are worth staying for.

Believe that. Please. Believe that.

And watch how everything changes when you do.
